Development of Modern Education and Press

Development of Education

Education Act of 1813
  • Misionary activists such as Charles Grant and William Wilberforce compelled East India Company to give up their non-involvement in education
  • Accrding to Charter act of 1813 the Governor-General was to keep a sum of 1 lakh for eduction
  • Hindu College (Presidency College) esatablished in 1817 with efforts of Raja Ram Mohan Roy and David Hare for imparting western education
  • Calcutta School Book Society established in 1817
  • Hare School established in 1818
  • Three Sanskrit colleges were set up at Calcutta

Committee of Public Instructions
  • Set up in 1823 to look after the development of education in India
  • Orientalists dominated the committee and supported the promotion of oriental learning
  • However differnt section from England and India put pressure on the East India Company to promote Western Education
  • The anglicist were supported by Lord Macaulay (Member of the executive council)
  • He wrote his famous Minute on Educational Policy on 2nd February 1835

Macaulay policy 1835
  • Persian was abolished as the court language and was substituted by English
  • Printing of English books was made free and was available at very low prices
  • It focused on educationg the upper strata of society through English, and leaving upto these people to promote vernacular language and literature
  • Calcutta Medical College, St. Xaviers College established in 1835
  • Bethune School was founded by JED Bethune in 1849

Wood's Dispatch 1854
  • Charles Wood prepared a dispatch on the educational system of India
  • Called Magna Carta of Education in the country
  • Government to spread Western education through English medium for higher education
  • Vernacular primary schools to be set up in rural areas
  • Grants-in-aid system to encourage private enterprises involvement in education
  • Promotion of education for women
  • Calcutta University established in 1857
  • Sir James William Colvile was its first principal

Hunter Commission 1882
  • Commission under William Wilson Hunter set up to evaluate progress achived under dispatch
  • Emphasised on states role in spreading primary education
  • Literary and practical learning in secondary level
  • Female education had been largely ignored
  • A whole hearted effort to involve private enterprises into education
  • Punjab university established in 1882
  • Allahab university established in 1887

Curzon's Reforms
  • Education was restricted by official control
  • Educated persons were motivaed to express loyalty to the British
  • For improving working of Indian Universities a commission was set up under Sir Thomas Raleigh

Indian Universities Act 1904
  • More stress was given to research activities
  • Strict regulation for affiliation to private colleges

Sadler Commission 1917
  • Appointed to study the problems of Calcutta university
  • Recommended a 12 year school course and 3 years college degree course
  • From 1916-1921 seven universities were set up
  • Education department was put in the hands of provincial ministries after the Montegu-Chelmsford reforms

Press Laws and Regulation

Censorship of press 1799
  • Imposed by lord Wellesly in 1799 under the apprehension of French attack in India
  • Imposed wartime restrictions on the press
  • Newspapres required to clearly print every issue, the name of the printer, the editor and the proprietor
  • Publisher to submit all material for pre-censorship
  • Restrictions were relaxed under Lord Hastings

Licensing regulation 1823
  • Promulgated by John Adams, it proved more stringent
  • Particularly aimed at Indian language newspapers and those edited by Indians
  • Every printer or published required a license for starting or using press
  • Penalty for publishing without requisite license was ₹ 400 or imprisonment
  • As a consequence Mirat-Ul-Akbar of Rammohan Roy has to stop publishing
  • This act was repealed by Charles Matcalfe in 1835, he is called Liberator of Indain Press

Licensing act of 1857
  • Imposed restrictions on the press on the ground of emergency due to the rebellion of 1857
  • Prohibited keeping or using a press without a license
  • Government reserved discretionary rights to grant or revoke license at any time

Vernacular Press act of 1878
  • To facilitate better control and to curb the vernacular press
  • Vernacular press was highly critical of the imperialistic activities of Lord Lytton
  • The act discriminated between English and vernacular press no right to appeal to acourt of law was given
  • In this context Amrita Bazar Patrika turned into an English newspaper overnight
  • Lord Ripon finally repealed it in 1882
