Post Gupta South India (535-1122 C.E)

Chalukyas And Pallavs

Chalukyas of Batapi
  • First capital was Batapi (in Bijapur)
  • 1st Pulakeshi (535 - 566) was the first free king
  • His deeds are known from the Batapi inscriptions
  • Kirtibarman was his successor
  • He extended his empire upto bihar and bengal,he also conquered Chola,Pandya
  • 2nd Pulakeshi (610-642) was the greatest king of this dynasty
  • He assumed titles of Ballabh, Prithivi-Ballabh, Parameshwar-Pram-Bhagawat
  • His deeds are known from Aihole inscriptions writen by his court poet Ravikirti
  • He defeated Harshavardhan
  • He defeated Pallav king Mahendravarman I
  • He was killed by Narasinghavarman I, son of 1st Mahendravarman
  • After his death Chalukya became weak and there was constant feus between Pallavas and Chalukyas
  • Many paintings and sculptures of the Ajanta and Ellora caves were completed during the reign of the Chalukyas
  • Chalukya power re-established during the reign of Vikramaditya I
  • His son Vikramaditya II (733-746 C.E) was the last powerful Chalukya ruler
  • In the middle of the eigth century the Rastrakuts became the major power in the south

Pallavs of Kanchi
  • Their history dates back to the 3rd century
  • Shivskandavarman was the first ruler
  • Political History of the Pallavs is known from the time of Singhavishnu (575-600 C.E)
  • Varabhi was his court poet
  • Chalukya-Pallav feud started from the reign of Mahendravarman I (600-630 C.E)
  • He wrote the sarcastic playMattavilas Prahasana in sanskrit
  • He assumed the titles of Gunavar, Bichitrachitta and Pallavamalla
  • He is also called Chaitakari due to the large number of temples built during his reign
  • He was succeeded by Narasinghavarman I (630-668 C.E)
  • He assumed the title of Mahamalla
  • He captured Chalukya capital of Batapi in 632 C.E and took the title of Batapikond
  • The famous Mahabalipuram temples were built during his reign
  • Narasinghavarman II was the next powerful ruler (695-722 C.E)
  • The last powerful ruler was Nandivarman II (780-796 C.E)
